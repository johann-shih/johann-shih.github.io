「Near-Futurism」: A New Thinking Paradigm

In subsequent dialogues with the large language model, we refined the basic framework of 「Near-Futurism」. The following summary is adapted based on the AI’s conclusions:

Summary of the 「Near-Futurism」 Ideological Framework

I. Core Definition

「Near-Futurism」 is a future-oriented, constructively-minded paradigm of thought and practical philosophy. It was born from a joint reflection on the utopian fantasies of modernism and the deconstructive impasse of postmodernism, aiming to transcend modernism’s “absolute truth” and postmodernism’s “deconstructive deadlock,” providing a complete operating system for this complex, uncertain era, one that combines critical wisdom, constructive agency, and profound ethical care.

Its fundamental stance is: On the basis of a deep understanding and respect for the limitations of historical and existing models, adopt a pragmatic, iterative, and responsible attitude to utilize all effective tools (regardless of their age) to build a more resilient and vibrant future, and serenely accept that its own outcomes will be iterated upon by the future.

II. Core Principles (The Four Pillars)

  1. Retrospective Understanding
    Rejects judging historical and existing knowledge systems through a simple “right/wrong” paradigm. Instead, it views them as valid models within their specific historical, technological, and social contexts, understanding their wisdom, their validity and value at the time, and their inevitable temporal limitations, drawing nourishment from them rather than simply discarding them.

  2. Present-Tense Building
    Takes pragmatism as its guideline for action. Focuses on solving current problems. The value of a theory, technology, or method is judged not by its novelty or ideological “purity,” but by its effectiveness in solving specific problems in the present.

  3. Iterative Progress
    Acknowledges that all current solutions (including its own) are provisional, optimizable “beta versions.” The development of knowledge and technology is an infinite, non-linear iterative process. The current best solution should be actively applied. It embraces feedback and change, viewing “being surpassed” as a natural outcome of success. It reserves open space for future revisions, upgrades, and even overhauls.

  4. Prospective Responsibility
    Requires actors to transcend short-term utilitarian calculations and be accountable for the long-term, systemic consequences and future impacts of their actions. We are not just actors in the present; we are “ancestors of the future,” capable of “giving an account and explanation” to future generations.

III. Key Methodologies and Advanced Connotations

  1. Everything is a Model: This is the worldview foundation of 「Near-Futurism」. It views all theories and knowledge as approximate models for explaining reality, thereby laying the philosophical groundwork for pragmatically switching, combining, and iterating them.
  2. Nonviolent Communication: This is the preferred communication method of 「Near-Futurism」. It transcends right/wrong criticism, delving into the needs behind actions to collaboratively create future solutions.
  3. Testing Mindset: This is its core methodology, transcending and encompassing critical thinking.
    • It demotes critical thinking from an ultimate goal (deconstruction) to a powerful diagnostic tool.
    • It adopts systematic testing (unit testing, integration testing, stress testing) similar to software engineering, proactively and constructively discovering the boundaries and flaws of models.
    • Its ultimate purpose is not negation, but iteration and optimization, completing the full cycle from “identifying problems” to “fixing and releasing.”
  4. Near-Future Social Contract: This is its ethical foundation, designed to avoid pure technological utilitarianism.
    • Contracting Parties are expanded through moral imagination to include contemporary people, future generations, and the non-human ecosystem.
    • Core elements include: The Right to Thrive supersedes the Right to Grow, The Right to the Unknown (adopting the precautionary principle), The Legacy Obligation (to pass on a resilient ecology and a commons of knowledge), and Iterative Ethics (ethics itself must also evolve).
    • It concretizes the “Prospective Responsibility” principle into an operable transgenerational ethical framework.

IV. Positioning Relative to Related Trends

  • Towards Modernism: It absorbs its constructive energy and hope, but discards its arrogance of “absolute truth” and linear view of progress.
  • Towards Postmodernism: It absorbs its critical spirit and skepticism, but transcends its nihilism of “deconstructing everything yet being powerless to rebuild,” turning towards constructive creation.
  • Towards Pragmatism: It places its “usefulness” criterion within a framework possessing greater temporal depth (retrospective and prospective) and ethical constraints (responsibility), becoming an upgraded version of pragmatism.
  • Towards Metamodernism: It resonates deeply with metamodernism and can be seen as a concretization of metamodernism in terms of philosophical and practical framework, particularly emphasizing its operability and ethical construction.

V. Practice Spectrum

「Near-Futurism」 is not just talk; it has clear practical manifestations in various fields:

Domain Core Practical Concept Specific Manifestations
Cognition & Comm. As Heuristic & Collaborative Tools Everything is a Model (Cognition), Nonviolent Comm. (Interpersonal)
Project Management Work in small, rapid iterations Agile Development
System Design Enhance adaptability & shock resistance Resilience Thinking, Antifragility
Philanthropy & Ethics Rational evaluation, utility maximization Effective Altruism
Social Governance Establish transgenerational ethics & governance frameworks Rule of Law, Jury System, Future Impact Assessment

VI. Summary: Portrait of a Near-Futurist

An individual or organization embodying 「Near-Futurism」 would be:

  • A Wise Inheritor: They can deeply understand the wisdom and limitations of old systems.
  • A Pragmatic Builder: They can flexibly use old and new tools to solve the most pressing current problems.
  • A Humble Iterator: They view their work as part of an ongoing process of progress and prepare the ground for its future replacement.
  • A Responsible Ancestor: Their every decision is imbued with deep care for distant descendants and the ecological home.
  • A Proactive Tester: They are not addicted in vague criticism, but actively design “tests,” constantly discovering and fixing flaws through construction.

Ultimately, 「Near-Futurism」 invites us to collectively practice a new maturity: After realizing that nothing is perfect, we still choose to engage in building with hope, and with the greatest wisdom and responsibility, lay the first solid cornerstone for our shared child—that future which has yet to arrive.
